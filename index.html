🧱 THE THREE NAMES FOR ONE TRUTHFUL TENSION

1. Phrygian Dominant (Middle Eastern, flamenco, modal power)

  • Scale: 1 – ♭2 – 3 – 4 – 5 – ♭6 – ♭7
  • In A#: A# – B – Cx – D# – E# – F# – G#

This is what you get from harmonic minor’s 5th mode, i.e., the V chord in Aeolian systems. It’s exotic, commanding, and used in modal/ethnic traditions—not so much for voice-leading, but for flavor.

Use this name when you believe in modal gravity and melodic identity.

2. Spanish Phrygian (Flamenco boys at the back of the bar)

  • Essentially the Phrygian Dominant, but with a stylistic bent.
  • Evokes Andalusian cadences, flamenco guitar, and percussive harmonic rhythm.
  • More cultural than theoretical—used to signal emotive performance practices.
Say this when you’re at a tablao and someone just played A7♯9♭13 with a tremolo roll.

3. Mixolydian ♯9 ♭13 (The jazzman's lens)

  • Implies a dominant chord with altered upper extensions.
  • No modal identity, just tensions added to a dominant root.
  • Used to voice chords, reharmonize melodies, and imply tonal function, not modal one.
  • Implies this chord is resolving somewhere (usually to a tonic minor), but the color is the focus.

In A#:
A# – Cx (♯9) – D# (4 or 11) – F# (♭13) – G# (♭7)
Missing the root or 3rd is fine; it’s all about voicing palette.

Say this when you're thinking about voice leading, tension stacks, and vertical color, not horizontal scale.

All three are looking at the same set of sounds—but their names reveal their priorities:

  • Do you care about melody or chord?
  • Do you believe in function or flavor?
  • Are you building a progression, a riff, or a moment?

And that’s why naming is myth-making.

To call it Mixolydian ♯9 ♭13 is to say: I know what this wants to do.
To call it Phrygian Dominant is to say: I know what this wants to be.
To call it Spanish Phrygian is to say: I know what this wants to feel like.

And who you are... is which of those truths you choose to serve.

what is Cx?
Cx is C double-sharp, enharmonically equivalent to D. It’s used to preserve diatonic spelling, especially in sharp keys. In A7♯9♭13, Cx is the ♯9—functionally important and theoretically correct even though it sounds like D.
