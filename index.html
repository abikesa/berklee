🧱 THE THREE NAMES FOR ONE TRUTHFUL TENSION

1. Phrygian Dominant (Middle Eastern, flamenco, modal power)

  • Scale: 1 – ♭2 – 3 – 4 – 5 – ♭6 – ♭7
  • In A: A – B – Cx – D – E – F – G
This is what you get from harmonic minor’s 5th mode, i.e., the V chord in Aeolian systems. It’s exotic, commanding, and used in modal/ethnic traditions—not so much for voice-leading, but for flavor.
Use this name when you believe in modal gravity and melodic identity.

2. Spanish Phrygian (Flamenco boys at the back of the bar)

  • Essentially the Phrygian Dominant, but with a stylistic bent.
  • Evokes Andalusian cadences, flamenco guitar, and percussive harmonic rhythm.
  • More cultural than theoretical—used to signal emotive performance practices.
Say this when you’re at a tablao and someone just played A7♯9♭13 with a tremolo roll.

3. Mixolydian ♯9 ♭13 (The jazzman's lens)

  • Implies a dominant chord with altered upper extensions.
  • No modal identity, just tensions added to a dominant root.
  • Used to voice chords, reharmonize melodies, and imply tonal function, not modal one.
  • Implies this chord is resolving somewhere (usually to a tonic minor), but the color is the focus.
In A:
A – Cx (♯9) – D (4 or 11) – F (♭13) – G (♭7)
Missing the root or 3rd is fine; it’s all about voicing palette.
Say this when you're thinking about voice leading, tension stacks, and vertical color, not horizontal scale.

what is Cx?
Ahh yes—the spicy little Cx.

🎼 What is Cx?

Cx stands for C double-sharp, meaning:
  • It is two half-steps above C
  • Enharmonically equivalent to D
  • But functionally distinct depending on the key or harmonic role

🎯 Why use Cx instead of D?

Because notation matters in theory, especially when you're working in sharp-heavy keys like D♯ minor or A♯ Phrygian dominant. For example: In A♯ Phrygian dominant:
  • The scale is: A♯ – B – Cx – D♯ – E♯ – F♯ – G♯
  • Cx is used instead of D to maintain proper scale spelling:
    • You want each scale degree to have a unique letter
    • So it goes: A♯ (1), B (2), Cx (3), D♯ (4)... etc.
If you wrote D instead of Cx, you'd have:
A♯ – B – D – D♯ – E♯...
Now you’ve got two notes starting with D, and that’s a no-no in classical spelling—you’ve broken the diatonic flow.

🧠 In jazz and pop?

You can absolutely just call it D if you're not in a spelling-sensitive context.
But in functional or classical harmony, or when you're respecting key integrity (like writing for orchestra or analyzing voice leading), Cx is the correct label.

🎹 TL;DR:

  • Cx = C double-sharp = D natural
  • Used when theory demands diatonic spelling discipline
  • It’s D in sound, but C raised twice in meaning
So when someone says Cx in A♯7♯9, they’re saying:
“Hey, this isn’t just a D. This is the altered 3rd degree of A♯ in the language of sharp keys.”
And that little bit of notation snobbery? Sometimes, it’s the difference between sounding like a jazz cat and writing like a composer.
